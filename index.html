Climate Change Impacts of Germany's Grid Infrastructure

Electricity grids are essential for integrating renewable electricity - but expanding grid infrastructure also causes additional emissions. Here, you can explore the results of our study on the environmental impacts of Germany's grid expansion, focusing on the climate change impact and what it originates from.

To see where we're coming from, let's start with the status quo - you can see the current figures on the right. To explore the environmental impacts, just scroll down.

The Carbon Footprint of Germany's Electricity Grid

Constructing Germany's grid infrastructure caused around 64 million tonnes of CO₂-equivalent emissions. This Sankey diagram shows how much each component contributes to the overall impact, what materials these impacts relate to and what processes are the actual emitters in the upstream supply chains.

To explore the connection, hover or click on parts of the Sankey diagram. If you scroll further down, we'll walk you through some results we find interesting.

The Elephant in the Room

Over 80% of the grid’s climate impact in 2023 came from conducting equipment — overhead lines (47.6%) and cables (35.9%). Aluminium is the main culprit here, with its high upstream electricity demand.

Masts also Matter

About a third of overhead line impacts come from masts, mainly due to iron and steel production. These emissions are largely direct (from blast furnaces), but electricity matters too.

The Role of Electricity Generation

Ironically, electricity production itself is one of the main contributors to the grid's carbon footprint — because it’s needed to produce aluminum, copper, steel, and insulation materials (plastics).

As the electricity mix decarbonizes in the future, the footprint of grid components will shrink. So, what to do?

Implications of Increasing Decarbonization

With more renewable electricity available, and, more broadly speaking, a general transition towards greener supply chains, the carbon footprint of grid components will decrease. At the same time, however, more grid infrastructure needs to be build to support the energy transition.
